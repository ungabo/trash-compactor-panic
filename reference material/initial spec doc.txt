# Trash Compactor Panic — Game Scope Document

## 1. Working Title

**Trash Compactor Panic**

## 2. Core Pitch

**Trash Compactor Panic** is a fast, funny, cooperative puzzle-action game where players are trapped inside a malfunctioning galactic garbage-processing chamber. The compactor walls are closing in, junk is flooding the room, pipes are clogging, trash categories must be sorted, and a ridiculous sewer creature keeps trying to eat the wrong things.

The player must survive each timed level by sorting junk, jamming the crusher, rerouting pipes, managing pressure, avoiding hazards, and coordinating character abilities before the room collapses.

The game should feel like a chaotic mix of:

* **Overcooked-style co-op chaos**
* **Bomberman-style top-down readability**
* **Light puzzle routing**
* **Timed arcade pressure**
* **Cartoon sci-fi parody humor**

The game should initially be built for **web** so gameplay timing, balance, screen layout, and content can be tuned quickly. The architecture should support later deployment to **Windows desktop** and **Android** without rebuilding core logic.

---

## 3. Target Platforms

### 3.1 Initial Platform

**Web browser first.**

Primary goals for the web build:

* Fast iteration
* Easy playtesting
* Tunable mechanics
* Keyboard controls first
* Responsive layouts
* Quick content updates
* Shareable prototype links

Recommended web target:

* Desktop browser first
* Later mobile browser support optional
* WebGL/canvas-based rendering preferred

### 3.2 Secondary Platforms

#### Windows

Windows should be treated as a packaged version of the mature web game unless a native engine is selected.

Possible approaches:

* **Godot export to Windows/Web/Android**
* **Unity export to WebGL/Windows/Android**
* **HTML5 game wrapped with Electron/Tauri for Windows**
* **Phaser/PixiJS web build wrapped later for desktop/mobile**

If the game starts in pure web tech, the desktop version should avoid depending on browser-only hacks that do not work reliably in wrappers.

#### Android

Android requires the most careful UI and control design because touch input, aspect ratio, screen size, and performance constraints differ heavily from desktop.

Android version should support:

* Touch controls
* Responsive HUD
* Pause/resume behavior
* Battery/performance considerations
* Larger tap targets
* Portrait or landscape decision early

Recommended initial Android orientation:

**Landscape only.**

Reason: the gameplay field, co-op-style movement, HUD, and pressure indicators fit much better in landscape. Portrait would require a fundamentally different layout and likely harm gameplay readability.

## 4. Genre and Player Experience

### 4.1 Genre

* Top-down / isometric puzzle-action arcade game
* Timed level-based progression
* Single-player with AI helpers or character switching
* Optional local co-op or online co-op as an expansion

### 4.2 Player Fantasy

The player is part of a bizarre cleanup/survival crew stuck inside a trash compactor. The fantasy is not heroic in a traditional sense. It is frantic, physical, and absurd:

* Sort garbage under pressure
* Throw junk into the right pipes
* Keep the crusher from closing
* Patch broken systems
* Keep a sewer monster from getting fed
* Yell internally while everything goes wrong

### 4.3 Tone

The tone should be funny, colorful, and chaotic, not dark or realistic.

Humor style:

* Workplace disaster comedy
* Sci-fi bureaucracy
* Trash-related absurdity
* Character-specific banter
* Fake safety warnings
* Overly formal instructions for ridiculous situations

Examples:

* “Do not feed the sewer thing.”
* “Organic waste goes in the green pipe. Screaming waste goes nowhere.”
* “Crusher pressure above 90% voids your employee survival warranty.”
* “Corporate thanks you for your temporary usefulness.”

## 5. Core Gameplay Loop

Each level follows a repeatable loop:

1. The level begins with a trash chamber layout.
2. Junk starts spawning from chutes, conveyors, or sludge pools.
3. The player identifies junk type and moves it to the correct destination.
4. The compactor walls slowly increase pressure.
5. The player uses crates, buttons, levers, pipes, and character abilities to delay or reduce pressure.
6. Hazards escalate: slime spills, pipe overloads, blocked routes, monster attacks, electrical surges.
7. The player completes the required objectives before the timer expires or pressure reaches failure.
8. The level ends with a grade based on speed, mistakes, damage, pressure control, and rescued valuables.

### 5.1 Moment-to-Moment Actions

The player should constantly make simple decisions:

* Pick up junk
* Throw junk
* Sort junk into the correct chute/pipe
* Push or pull crates
* Stand on pressure plates
* Activate switches
* Repair broken panels
* Reroute pipe valves
* Avoid slime, crushing walls, laser hazards, or monster tentacles
* Use character abilities
* Decide whether to solve objectives or clean up extra mess for score

The actions should be simple. The complexity should come from time pressure, layout, hazards, and overlapping objectives.

## 6. Main Game Modes

### 6.1 Campaign Mode

A structured progression of levels across different garbage-processing chambers.

Campaign should introduce mechanics gradually:

1. Basic movement and pickup/throw
2. Sorting trash types
3. Wall pressure
4. Jam plates
5. Pipes and valves
6. Character abilities
7. Sewer monster
8. Multi-room levels
9. Hazard combinations
10. Boss-style compactor events

Campaign progression unlocks:

* New characters
* New chambers
* New trash types
* New hazards
* New tools
* Cosmetic room themes
* Higher difficulty variants

### 6.2 Arcade Mode

A quick-play mode where the player survives increasingly difficult waves of trash and compactor pressure.

Arcade goals:

* High score chasing
* Endless or semi-endless mode
* Good for web demo
* Lower content requirements than full campaign

### 6.3 Challenge Mode

Short handcrafted levels built around one mechanic.

Examples:

* Sort 30 items with no mistakes
* Survive 2 minutes without jamming the compactor
* Reroute all pipes while avoiding slime
* Keep the sewer monster unfed
* Complete level using only one character

### 6.4 Co-op Mode

Co-op can be implemented later, but the game design should account for it early.

Possible co-op forms:

* Local same-keyboard co-op on web/Windows
* Local controller co-op on Windows
* Online co-op later
* Android local co-op is lower priority due to screen/input limitations

For first build, implement:

* Single-player with character switching
* Optional AI helper behavior later

## 7. Player Characters

The game should use a small cast of distinct, readable characters. Each has a clear silhouette, role, ability, and personality.

### 7.1 P1 Bucket — Grab & Toss Specialist

**Role:** Fast trash mover.

Visual identity:

* Raccoon-like or masked alien scavenger
* Small, quick, energetic
* Tool belt, gloves, maybe a bucket helmet or trash-picker tool

Core ability:

* Can pick up and throw junk farther than others
* Throws in an arc
* Can toss light crates over hazards

Gameplay use:

* Fast sorting
* Emergency disposal
* Tossing items to teammates
* Feeding or distracting the monster with decoy junk

Weakness:

* Poor at repairing machinery
* Cannot move heavy objects alone

### 7.2 P2 Zappa — Power Surge Specialist

**Role:** Electrical control and short-range stun.

Visual identity:

* Green alien with antennae or glowing fingertips
* Slightly chaotic personality
* Purple electric effects

Core ability:

* Sends a power surge into panels, doors, and broken machinery
* Can temporarily stun the sewer creature or disable a hazard

Gameplay use:

* Activate dead panels
* Repair overloaded power nodes
* Stop crusher movement briefly
* Open electrified gates

Weakness:

* Slower movement
* Can overload systems if used repeatedly without cooldown

### 7.3 P3 Glitch — Hacker / Routing Specialist

**Role:** Pipe rerouting and system hacking.

Visual identity:

* Small robot with screen face
* Nervous, literal, and overly helpful

Core ability:

* Hacks consoles
* Changes pipe routing
* Opens locked gates
* Reveals hidden trash labels or hazard warnings

Gameplay use:

* Solve routing puzzles
* Control conveyors
* Redirect trash streams
* View mini-map details

Weakness:

* Weak physical strength
* Cannot throw heavy junk far
* Vulnerable while hacking

### 7.4 P4 M.O.B. — Pipe Butler / Heavy Utility

**Role:** Heavy lifter and pipe manager.

Visual identity:

* Blob-like or bulky maintenance servant
* Formal bow tie or vest
* Polite and strangely dignified

Core ability:

* Pushes heavy crates
* Carries large junk items
* Turns heavy valves faster
* Can block slime flow temporarily

Gameplay use:

* Jam plates
* Heavy item transport
* Pipe maintenance
* Emergency path clearing

Weakness:

* Slow movement
* Poor at precise tasks

## 8. Control Schemes

Controls must be planned separately for web/keyboard, Windows/controller, and Android/touch.

### 8.1 Web Keyboard Controls

Default single-player controls:

* Move: WASD or arrow keys
* Pick up / drop: Space or E
* Throw: Hold and release Space or mouse button
* Interact: E
* Character switch: Tab or number keys 1–4
* Ability: Q or right mouse button
* Dash: Shift
* Pause: Esc

Alternative:

* Mouse click-to-move could be considered later but should not be required.

### 8.2 Windows Controller Controls

Recommended controller mapping:

* Move: Left stick
* Aim throw: Right stick or movement direction
* Pick up / drop: A
* Throw: Hold/release A or right trigger
* Interact: X
* Ability: B or left trigger
* Character switch: Shoulder buttons
* Dash: Right bumper
* Pause: Menu button

### 8.3 Android Touch Controls

Recommended Android layout:

* Landscape orientation
* Virtual joystick bottom-left
* Context action button bottom-right
* Ability button near action button
* Character switch buttons along bottom or side
* Throw direction via drag-and-release from action button
* Pause/settings top-right

Touch design requirements:

* Tap targets at least 48 dp
* Avoid critical buttons near Android gesture/navigation zones
* Allow repositioning controls if possible
* Keep important gameplay area unobstructed
* HUD must scale down intelligently

### 8.4 Cross-Platform Input Architecture

The game should use an input abstraction layer.

Core actions should be named abstractly:

* Move
* PickUp
* Drop
* ThrowStart
* ThrowRelease
* Interact
* Ability
* SwitchCharacterNext
* SwitchCharacterPrevious
* Pause

Do not hard-code gameplay logic directly to keyboard keys or touch buttons.

## 9. Camera and Perspective

### 9.1 Recommended Perspective

Use a **top-down angled/isometric-style camera**.

Benefits:

* Shows room layout clearly
* Supports four-direction movement
* Works well for puzzle-action gameplay
* Makes compactors, pipes, crates, and hazards readable
* Scales better across platforms than full 3D camera control

### 9.2 Camera Behavior

For single-player:

* Camera follows the active character
* Slight smoothing
* Keep objective-critical areas visible where possible
* Zoom out during emergency events
* Do not rotate camera during normal gameplay

For co-op:

* Camera may frame all active players if local co-op
* If players separate too far, either limit separation or zoom out

### 9.3 Level Framing

Levels should be designed to fit within common screen ratios:

* 16:9 desktop
* 16:10 laptop
* 21:9 ultrawide
* 4:3 fallback/tablet-ish layouts
* Android landscape ratios from roughly 16:9 to 20:9

Avoid placing critical UI or gameplay objects only at extreme edges.

## 10. Screen Ratio and Platform Layout Strategy

This is important. The game should not be designed only for one fixed screenshot layout.

### 10.1 Safe Gameplay Area

Define a central **safe gameplay area** where core action occurs.

* Must remain visible on all supported ratios
* Should not be covered by HUD
* Level geometry should avoid critical mechanics at the farthest screen edges

### 10.2 HUD Zones

Recommended HUD zones:

#### Desktop / Web 16:9

* Top: pressure meter, timer, level status
* Left: character list / objectives compact panel
* Right: active objectives / warnings / mini-map
* Bottom: inventory, ability bar, interaction prompts

#### Desktop Ultrawide

* Keep gameplay centered
* Side panels can expand outward
* Do not stretch the gameplay camera too far horizontally if it exposes unfinished level edges
* Optional decorative background or extended UI panels can fill side space

#### Laptop 16:10

* Similar to 16:9
* Bottom HUD may need to be shorter
* Keep buttons compact

#### Android Landscape

* Top: pressure/timer compact bar
* Bottom-left: joystick
* Bottom-right: action buttons
* Character switch buttons should be compact icons
* Objectives should collapse into expandable panel
* Mini-map should be optional/collapsible

### 10.3 Responsive UI Rules

The UI should have breakpoints:

#### Large Desktop

* Full HUD visible
* Objective list expanded
* Tooltips and text descriptions available

#### Small Desktop / Tablet Landscape

* Compact panels
* Smaller objective descriptions
* Icons plus short labels

#### Android Landscape

* Essential UI only
* Hide long text during gameplay
* Use icon-first design
* Show details only when paused or when a panel is opened

### 10.4 Text Scaling

Text must remain legible:

* Minimum body/UI text on desktop: ~14 px equivalent
* Minimum touch UI text: ~16 dp equivalent
* Avoid tiny labels on Android
* Use icons and color coding where possible

### 10.5 Aspect Ratio Testing Requirements

Developer should test at minimum:

* 1920×1080
* 1366×768
* 1440×900
* 2560×1440
* 3440×1440
* 1280×720
* 2340×1080 Android landscape
* 2400×1080 Android landscape
* 2560×1600 tablet landscape

## 11. Main Resources and Meters

### 11.1 Wall Pressure

Wall pressure is the primary failure meter.

Behavior:

* Starts low
* Increases over time
* Increases faster when trash piles up
* Increases when pipes clog
* Decreases or pauses when compactor is jammed
* Critical zone causes alarms, screen shake, and faster hazard events

Suggested states:

* 0–50%: Safe
* 51–75%: Warning
* 76–90%: Danger
* 91–99%: Critical
* 100%: Failure unless emergency save active

### 11.2 Time Left

Some levels have a fixed timer.

Timer roles:

* Complete objective before time runs out
* Survive until extraction
* Hold pressure below threshold for a duration

### 11.3 Junk Overflow

Each chute or pipe can have an overflow meter.

Overflow consequences:

* Wrong junk causes clogs
* Clogs increase wall pressure
* Full chute spills junk back into room
* Toxic overflow creates hazard tiles

### 11.4 Character Health / Stamina

Avoid complex health early. Better initial system:

* Characters get stunned briefly by hazards
* No permanent death in early levels
* Stunned characters drop carried item
* Severe mistakes cost score or time

For advanced modes:

* Shared crew safety meter
* Individual stamina for abilities

### 11.5 Score

Score rewards:

* Correct sorting
* Fast completion
* Low pressure
* Few mistakes
* Bonus valuables rescued
* No monster feeding
* Clean finish

Score penalties:

* Wrong sorting
* Feeding sewer creature
* Letting trash overflow
* Character stun
* Broken machinery ignored

## 12. Trash Types

Trash must be visually readable at a glance.

Each item needs:

* Clear silhouette
* Color-coded category
* Optional icon marker
* Weight class
* Hazard properties
* Correct destination

### 12.1 Basic Trash Categories

#### Recyclables — Blue

Examples:

* Metal cans
* Scrap panels
* Broken droid parts
* Empty coolant tubes
* Space bottles

Destination:

* Blue recycling chute or pipe

#### Organics — Green

Examples:

* Alien food scraps
* Rotten space fruit
* Bio-sludge sacks
* Fungal clumps
* Meat cubes of unclear origin

Destination:

* Green organics pipe

#### Toxic — Purple

Examples:

* Leaking battery cells
* Glowing ooze barrels
* Radioactive cubes
* Chemical sacks
* Mutant slime jars

Destination:

* Purple toxic pipe

#### General Junk — Gray / Brown

Examples:

* Broken chairs
* Crates
* Random machinery chunks
* Bent pipes
* Packaging waste

Destination:

* General compactor chute
* Sometimes used for jam plates

### 12.2 Advanced Trash Types

#### Explosive Junk

* Must be moved carefully
* Explodes if thrown too hard or left near sparks
* Can clear slime or stun monster if used strategically

#### Valuable Junk

* Optional bonus items
* Must be moved to rescue bin, not trash chute
* Adds score or unlocks cosmetics

#### Monster Bait

* Attracts sewer creature
* Can be used as distraction
* Wrong use causes monster to grow or block paths

#### Magnetic Junk

* Sticks to metal surfaces or other junk
* May require special tool or character

#### Heavy Junk

* Requires M.O.B. or two characters
* Can block pathways
* Useful for jam plates

## 13. Core Mechanics

### 13.1 Pick Up and Carry

Characters can pick up items if they are within range and the item is not too heavy.

Rules:

* Light junk: all characters can carry
* Medium junk: most characters can carry, slower movement
* Heavy junk: only M.O.B. or two-character interaction
* Hazardous junk: may require special handling or time limit

### 13.2 Throwing

Throwing should be simple and readable.

Controls:

* Hold action to aim
* Release to throw
* Quick tap drops item

Throw behavior:

* Light items travel far
* Heavy items travel short
* Items bounce slightly
* Wrong throws can knock into hazards or monster

### 13.3 Sorting

Junk must be delivered to the correct chute, pipe, or bin.

Sorting feedback:

* Correct sort: satisfying sound, points, small sparkle/pop
* Wrong sort: buzzer, clog risk, warning icon
* Repeated wrong sort: pipe overload

### 13.4 Jam Plates

Jam plates are numbered pressure platforms that delay or stop the compactor.

Rules:

* Place correct weight/object on plate
* Some plates need crates
* Some plates need specific trash type
* Some plates require multiple plates activated simultaneously
* Jam duration may be temporary

Example objective:

* “Jam the compactor with 3 crates: 0/3”

### 13.5 Wall Compactor

The walls are the main threat.

Behavior:

* Walls slowly move inward as pressure rises
* At danger states, wall movement becomes visible
* Objects near walls may be crushed
* Crushed junk can become compacted blocks
* Players caught near walls are stunned or fail the level depending on difficulty

### 13.6 Pipe Rerouting

Pipes carry sorted trash out of the room.

Mechanic:

* Pipes have color-coded routes
* Valves can redirect routes
* Some pipes clog
* Some routes temporarily close
* Wrong junk into pipe increases clog meter

Glitch should interact with pipe consoles faster than others.

### 13.7 Valves

Valves are physical interaction points.

Use cases:

* Open pipe route
* Close overflow route
* Drain slime
* Redirect toxic waste
* Lower wall pressure

M.O.B. can turn heavy valves faster.

### 13.8 Hacking Panels

Hacking panels are short mini-interactions, not long puzzle screens during active chaos.

Possible designs:

* Hold interact while a progress bar fills
* Quick directional sequence
* Connect two nodes on a small grid
* Match symbol/color route

For web-first prototype, start with a simple hold-to-hack progress bar.

### 13.9 Emergency Jam

Emergency Jam is a limited-use panic button.

Effect:

* Freezes wall movement briefly
* Reduces pressure slightly
* Causes a cooldown
* May create more junk afterward as side effect

Use:

* Player bailout mechanic
* Prevents frustration
* Adds dramatic timing

### 13.10 Sewer Creature

The sewer creature is a recurring hazard.

Behavior:

* Lurks in sludge around the room
* Tentacles reach into the play area
* Eats junk if fed
* Gets angry if toxic waste is mishandled
* Blocks paths temporarily
* Can grab items or characters

Mechanics:

* Feed decoy bait to distract it
* Stun with Zappa’s power surge
* Avoid letting it eat valuable or wrong junk
* It may spit junk back into the room

Failure consequence:

* Monster fed too much = pressure spike, hazard spawn, or level failure in challenge mode

## 14. Hazards

### 14.1 Slime Pools

* Slow movement
* May spread from broken pipes
* Can be cleaned or drained
* Toxic slime stuns characters briefly

### 14.2 Electrical Sparks

* Appear near broken panels
* Stun characters
* Can ignite explosive junk
* Zappa can temporarily control or repair them

### 14.3 Crusher Walls

* Move inward
* Destroy objects
* Can block routes
* Major pressure threat

### 14.4 Laser Scanners

* Sweep across the floor
* Stun characters or trigger alarms
* Can be disabled by hacking

### 14.5 Conveyor Belts

* Move junk automatically
* Can help or hurt
* Direction can be changed with panels

### 14.6 Trash Floods

* Sudden wave of junk from chute
* Pushes items and players
* Can overload sorting routes

### 14.7 Pipe Bursts

* Burst pipe sprays slime or steam
* Blocks passage
* Can be fixed by valve or tool

## 15. Level Objectives

Each level should have 1–3 primary objectives and optional bonus objectives.

### 15.1 Primary Objective Examples

* Sort 20 junk items correctly
* Jam the compactor with 3 crates
* Keep pressure below 90%
* Reroute all pipes
* Survive for 3 minutes
* Rescue 5 valuables
* Prevent the sewer creature from eating toxic junk
* Repair 4 broken panels
* Drain all sludge before extraction

### 15.2 Optional Bonus Objectives

* No wrong sorts
* Finish under target time
* Keep pressure below 70%
* No character stunned
* Sort all valuables
* Do not use Emergency Jam
* Keep monster hunger below 50%

## 16. Level Design

### 16.1 Level Structure

A level should include:

* Spawn points for junk
* Sorting destinations
* Movement paths
* Pressure plates
* Hazard sources
* Repair or hack panels
* Optional bonus item locations
* Safe zones
* Emergency route options

### 16.2 Early Level Design Principles

Early levels should be compact and readable.

Do not introduce too many systems at once.

Example early progression:

#### Level 1 — Sorting Day

* One room
* Three trash types
* No compactor movement at first
* Teach pickup, carry, sort

#### Level 2 — The Walls Have Opinions

* Introduce wall pressure
* One jam plate
* One crate

#### Level 3 — Pipe Dream

* Introduce pipe color routing
* One valve
* Wrong sorting causes clogs

#### Level 4 — Something in the Sludge

* Introduce sewer creature
* Teach distraction and avoidance

#### Level 5 — Teamwork Required

* Introduce character switching
* Need Bucket and Glitch abilities

### 16.3 Level Size

Prototype levels should be small:

* 1 to 2 screens worth of space
* Minimal scrolling early
* Clear routes

Later levels can expand:

* Multi-room layouts
* Locked doors
* Conveyor corridors
* Split objectives

## 17. Difficulty Design

Difficulty should come from overlapping simple problems, not complicated controls.

### 17.1 Difficulty Variables

* Trash spawn rate
* Number of trash types
* Pressure increase speed
* Number of hazards
* Pipe clog speed
* Monster aggression
* Objective count
* Route complexity
* Time limit
* Mistake tolerance

### 17.2 Difficulty Modes

#### Casual

* Slower pressure
* More forgiving wrong sorts
* Longer timers
* More tutorial prompts

#### Standard

* Intended default
* Moderate pressure
* Mistakes matter but are recoverable

#### Panic

* Faster pressure
* Less warning time
* More hazards
* Higher score multiplier

#### Endless

* Escalating difficulty until failure

## 18. UI and HUD

### 18.1 Required Gameplay HUD Elements

* Wall pressure meter
* Timer or survival duration
* Active objectives
* Character selector/status
* Carried item indicator
* Ability cooldowns
* Emergency Jam button/status
* Mini-map or room overview for larger levels
* Warning alerts

### 18.2 HUD Priority

During active gameplay, show only what matters immediately:

Highest priority:

* Pressure
* Timer
* Current objective
* Current character
* Carried item
* Ability cooldown
* Danger alerts

Lower priority:

* Score details
* Bonus objectives
* Full tutorial text
* Detailed character stats
* Mini-map on small screens

### 18.3 Warning System

Warnings should be visual and audio:

* Pressure rising
* Wall movement imminent
* Pipe clogging
* Wrong trash type
* Monster approaching
* Hazard active
* Objective almost failed

Warnings should use:

* Flashing border
* Icon above hazard
* Short text callout
* Sound cue
* Optional controller/touch vibration on supported platforms

### 18.4 Pause Screen

Pause screen should include:

* Resume
* Restart level
* Controls
* Objectives
* Settings
* Exit to level select

### 18.5 Results Screen

After each level:

* Completion grade
* Time
* Correct sorts
* Wrong sorts
* Highest pressure reached
* Monster incidents
* Bonus objectives completed
* Coins/scrap earned
* Unlocks

* Chunky silhouettes
* Bright color coding
* Expressive characters

### 19.2 Camera Readability

Objects must be identifiable from gameplay distance.

Requirements:

* Trash categories color-coded
* Hazards animated clearly
* Character outlines distinct
* Important interactables glow or pulse subtly
* Wall pressure visually communicated by wall movement, lights, and UI

## 21. Progression and Rewards

### 21.1 Level Grades

Possible grades:

* C: survived
* B: completed with some mistakes
* A: good time and pressure control
* S: all bonus objectives

### 21.2 Unlocks

Unlock types:

* New characters
* Character skins
* New tools
* New chamber themes
* New challenge levels
* Funny safety posters
* Cosmetic trash effects

### 21.3 Currency

Use a simple currency such as **Scrap Tokens**.

Earned from:

* Level completion
* Bonus objectives
* Valuable junk saved
* Arcade milestones

Spent on:

* Cosmetic unlocks
* Optional ability upgrades
* Challenge modifiers

Be careful with upgrades. The game should remain skill-based. Upgrades should not break level balance.

## 22. Multiplayer and Co-op Scope

### 22.1 Recommended First Build

Start with **single-player character switching**.

Reason:

* Easier to build
* Easier to tune
* Same mechanics can later support co-op
* Avoids networking complexity early

### 22.2 Local Co-op Later

Local co-op is a strong fit for Windows and desktop web.

Implementation considerations:

* Keyboard sharing is awkward but possible
* Controller support is better
* Camera must handle multiple players
* UI must show multiple player statuses

### 22.3 Online Co-op Later

Online co-op is not recommended for the first complete version unless the developer already has multiplayer expertise.

Online adds:

* Latency handling
* Syncing physics/items
* Reconnects
* Matchmaking/lobbies
* Cross-platform input differences
* More QA burden

Architecture should not block it, but it should not be a first-version dependency.

## 23. Technical Architecture Recommendation

### 23.1 Engine Options

#### Option A — Godot

Strong candidate.

Pros:

* Exports to web, Windows, Android
* Good 2D support
* Lightweight
* Friendly for top-down games
* Scene/node system fits this type of game

Cons:

* Web export can have browser quirks
* Android export setup requires care
* Asset pipeline must be organized well

#### Option B — Unity

Also viable.

Pros:

* Mature export pipeline
* Strong tooling
* Good Android/Windows support
* Large developer ecosystem

Cons:

* Heavier
* WebGL builds can be large
* More overhead for a simple 2D game

#### Option C — Phaser / PixiJS Web First

Good for fast web prototyping.

Pros:

* Very fast browser iteration
* Great for 2D web games
* Easy deployment

Cons:

* Android and Windows require wrappers or rework
* Touch/controller handling must be planned carefully
* Performance depends heavily on browser/device

### 23.2 Practical Recommendation

If the goal is truly Windows + Android + Web, use **Godot** or **Unity** rather than a web-only framework.

If the goal is fastest concept testing, use **Phaser/PixiJS** for prototype, but expect extra work later.

Best compromise:

* Build prototype in Godot with Web export from day one
* Keep rendering 2D/isometric
* Avoid engine-specific hacks
* Export web first for playtesting
* Later export Windows and Android

## 33. Game Feel Requirements

The game must feel responsive.

Important details:

* Pickups should snap quickly
* Throwing should feel punchy
* Correct sorting should feel satisfying
* Pressure increases should feel tense but fair
* Mistakes should be obvious
* Hazards should telegraph before hitting
* Character switching should be fast
* Emergency Jam should feel powerful

Avoid:

* Sluggish movement
* Hard-to-select items
* Overlapping interact prompts
* Tiny collision boxes
* Unclear trash category visuals
* Failure without warning

---

## 34. Failure States

Possible failures:

* Wall pressure reaches 100%
* Timer expires
* Critical objective fails
* Monster consumes forbidden item
* Too many wrong sorts

Failure screen should be funny, not punishing.

Example messages:

* “Compacted with enthusiasm.”
* “The trash won.”
* “Corporate has promoted your remains to mulch.”
* “The sewer thing has filed a complaint.”

---

## 35. Monetization

No monetization is required for the core scope.

If monetization is ever considered:

* Avoid pay-to-win
* Cosmetic-only unlocks are safest
* Premium full-game purchase is cleaner than ads/IAP for this kind of game

For now, monetization should not drive design decisions.

## 36. Legal / IP Positioning

This should be developed as an original sci-fi comedy game.

Avoid:

* Specific Star Wars names
* Recognizable characters
* Recognizable uniforms
* Recognizable ships
* Specific logos/symbols
* Direct scene recreation
* Famous dialogue or music references

Use broad genre parody instead:

* Evil space corporations
* Generic battle stations
* Cosmic garbage rooms
* Bucket-helmet minions
* Alien maintenance crews
* Absurd bureaucracy

## 40. Success Criteria

The prototype is successful if:

* The player understands what to do within 30 seconds
* Sorting trash feels clear and satisfying
* Wall pressure creates tension without feeling unfair
* The jam plate creates meaningful decision-making
* The UI remains readable at common desktop sizes
* The game loop is fun enough to replay after losing
* The mechanics can plausibly expand into character abilities, pipe routing, and hazards

The full game is successful if it delivers fast, funny, readable chaos without overwhelming the player with unclear systems.
